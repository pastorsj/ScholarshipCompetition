Understanding United States Contracts

In 2006, the Federal Funding Accountability and Transparency Act was signed into law and required that federal contract, loan, and other financial assistance awards of more than $25,000 be displayed on a publicly accessible and searchable website to give the American people access to information on how their tax dollars were being spent. Through a wide variety of processes, the US government ensures the quality of the data sourced through multiple internal data systems. Here, the focus is looking at the investments made by the government through contracts and the subsequent investments in subcontracts. Contracts are an agreement creating mutual obligations between parties that is enforceable by law. Government contracts specifically are contracts between the US government and businesses that "obligate the seller to provide the supplied or serivces (including construction) and the buyer (the US Government) to pay for them" (USA Spending).
In a recent executive order from the Biden Administration, cybersecurity standards for United States defense contracts became much more rigorous. However, according to an article in CPO Magazine (insert link), "a survey of 300 small-to-medium business defense contractors found common failures to comply with CMMC requirements, with 48% having severse vunerabiltiies" (CPO). This is extremely concerning on the National Security front, where not only does the US governmnent need to worrty about themselves being vunerabvle to cyber attacks, but they also need to worry about their defense contractors being vunerable. Recently, a list of critical and emerging technologies was announced by the Fast Track Action Subcommittee on Critical and Emerging Technologies (CETs) of the National Science and Technology Council. This list included Artificial Intelligence, Biotechnologies, Quantum Information Technologies, and Hypersonics. In particular, hypersonics, is of great interest to the Department of Defense. Hypersonics generally applies to weapons that travel in excess of five times the speed of sound and are hard to stop. Combining these concerns and national security interests, the National Security Agency is prioritizing new methods of determining contractors that are critical in the supply chain and therefore need to boost their cybersecurity protection.
There are two steps to determining the businesses that need to prioritize improving their cybersecurity protection. First, a list of all of the recent contracts and contractors needs to be filtered down to only those that provide a specific type of technologies (based on the CETs). Second, for every contractor, a list of subcontractors must be retrieved and filtered down to only those that supply specific capabilties or products to the prime contractor. In the following analysis, the focus is primarily on the second step.

Visualizing Subcontracts

In 2016, the Department of Defense awarded a contract to Raytheon for the second phase of the Hypersonic Air-Breathing Weapon Concept. Raytheon is one of the largest "aerospace, intellegence services providers, and defense manufacturers in the world". According to the USA Spending website, they have been awarded over $4.7 billion in contracts, primarily from the Department of Defense, and primarily for products like guided missles and bombs (https://www.usaspending.gov/recipient/01c4a3a3-b4c5-ce4e-822b-d17f09985001-C/latest). Clearly, from the contract description and the company that was awarded the contract, Raytheon is an important company that the US government should have a vested interest in protecting from cybersecurity threats. However, according to the contract, there are 348 subcontractors that supply products and services to Raytheon, but it is not yet clear which subcontractors should be prioritized in the same way that Raytheon will be. The following sections will visualize the subcontractor data in such a way so that it will become clear which subcontractors are more important in the supply chain. First, in Figure 1, a very basic timeline shows the Raytheon contract and a couple of subcontracts awarded around the time of the official contract.
Using the above timeline, there are a number of important dates and subcontracts that stood out immeditely when looking at the data manually from a high level. First, the Raytheon contract started in October of 2016 and ended as of March 2022. Before the contract was awarded, a number of large subcontracts were awarded to Alliant TechSystems, which upon closer inspection and further research, is tied to Northrup Grumman, a well known American aerospace and defense company. These subcontracts turn out to be far larger than any of the other subcontracts, so this is the first indication that this subcontractor is important. Lastly, after doing a brief search through the subcontracts, there is a clear indication that some of these business provide critical supplies for building hypersonic devices. Two subcontracts are shown, one that provides batteries (ATB) and one that provides accelerometers and cables (Dytran).
To dive deeper into the timeline and get our first sense of all of the subcontracts, a second timeline chart shows the dates when a contract or multiple contracts were awarded by Raytheon. To explore this visualization, click on one of the orange dots. It will show a second chart with information on all of the contracts awarded on that date.
In Figure 2, the viewer gets the first taste of the progression all of the subcontracts awarded during the time of the Raytheon contract. This progression could be tracable to the steps for producing the hypersonic missles. For example, the earlier subcontracts would be important as they are supplying parts for the missles, while the later subcontracts would be for testing and validation purposes.

Exploring Subcontract Funding

After looking at the timeline and a brief preview of the subcontracts, it seems that two pathes should be taken to determine the importance of a subcontract. One indication could be the cost of the subcontract. Outlying subcontracts that cost far more than other subcontracts could be a good indicator. Secondly, contracts that supply specific parts that are used to build hypersonic missles are equally important. First, let's take a closer look at well funded subcontracts.
